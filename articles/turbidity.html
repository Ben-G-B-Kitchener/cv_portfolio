Turbidity Measurement and Suspended Sediment Research

Ben G. B. Kitchener

Turbidity measurement is widely used as a practical proxy for suspended sediment concentration, water quality, and optical clarity. However, turbidity is not a direct physical measurement of sediment concentration. It is an optical response that depends on particle size, particle shape, refractive index, wavelength, detector geometry, and the optical design of the instrument.

My research interest in turbidity developed through work on suspended sediment monitoring and the design of scientific instrumentation for laboratory and field-scale sediment research. This included the development of low-cost turbidity measurement hardware and a review of the physical principles behind turbidity measurement.

Why turbidity is difficult to interpret

Turbidity instruments do not measure sediment mass directly. They measure scattered or transmitted light, which is then related to a calibration standard or empirical sediment concentration model.

This means that two water samples with the same suspended sediment concentration can produce different turbidity readings if their particle size distributions, mineralogy, colour, or organic content differ.

Instrument geometry matters

Different turbidity instruments use different optical geometries. Some measure light transmitted through the sample, while others measure light scattered at an angle. Nephelometric instruments commonly use detection near 90 degrees, but this is only one possible measurement geometry.

The geometry of the light source, detector, sample path, and wavelength selection all influence the instrument response.
